Problem.

Does there exist an element $\alpha \in \mathbb{F}_{q^n}$ that is normal over every intermediate field $\mathbb{F}_{q^d}$ with $d \mid n$?

Definition.

Such an element is called completely normal over $\mathbb{F}_q$. Equivalently, $\alpha$ is completely normal if it is a normal element of $\mathbb{F}_{q^n}$ over $\mathbb{F}_{q^d}$ for every divisor $d$ of $n$.

Status.

This existence problem is solved: for every finite extension $\mathbb{F}_{q^n}/\mathbb{F}_q$, there exists a completely normal element.

If $\alpha$ is completely normal over $\mathbb{F}_q$, then its minimal polynomial over $\mathbb{F}_q$ is an irreducible polynomial of degree $n$. One may call this a completely normal polynomial in the sense that one of its roots is completely normal.

Remark.

This is different from the primitive completely normal problem. Requiring $\alpha$ to be both primitive and completely normal leads to the Morgan-Mullen conjecture, which is substantially harder and is recorded separately in `primitive completely normal problem.md`.

References.

D. Blessenohl and K. Johnsen, Eine Verschärfung des Satzes von der Normalbasis, J. Algebra 103 (1986), 141-159.

D. Hachenberger, Finite Fields: Normal Bases and Completely Free Elements, Kluwer Academic Publishers, 1997.
