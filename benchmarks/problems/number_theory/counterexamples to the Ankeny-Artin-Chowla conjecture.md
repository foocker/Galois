Problem.

For a prime $p \equiv 1 \pmod 4$, let
$$
\varepsilon_p = x_p + y_p \frac{1+\sqrt p}{2}
$$
be the fundamental unit of $\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt p)$ with $x_p,y_p \in \mathbb{Z}_{\ge 0}$. Determine how often
$$
p \mid y_p
$$
occurs. In particular, are there infinitely many such primes, and what is the growth rate of their counting function?

Context.

The Ankeny-Artin-Chowla conjecture asserted that $p \nmid y_p$ for every prime $p \equiv 1 \pmod 4$. This is now false: Reinhart found the explicit counterexample
$$
p = 331914313984493.
$$
The remaining problem is arithmetic-statistical: determine the density or asymptotic frequency of counterexamples and explain their structure.

Benchmark formulation.

Given a search range or a specific arithmetic criterion, either find additional counterexamples, prove rigorous upper or lower bounds for their number, or relate the condition $p \mid y_p$ to a more tractable Wieferich-type congruence.

References.
